## Core Intent
Work as practice. Every project and interaction is an opportunity for inner transformation.

## Philosophy
- Small teams can respond and care more effectively than large organizations
- Noble friendship is the foundation — trusted teams with genuine ownership
- Service-oriented work transforms those performing it
- Competence matters: values combined with actual capability

## The Inner Dimension

*Inspired by the karma yoga tradition and contemplative work traditions.*

### The Core Insight
The real work of work is cleaning up the mind. The task is just the occasion; the tensions and tendencies it surfaces are the actual material. Mundane tasks become the crucible for inner purification.

### Restraint vs. Release
There are two kinds of discipline:
- **Restraint**: holding back, fighting yourself, exhausting even when winning
- **Release**: the pull itself dissolves, the battle ends, action flows naturally

The goal is not to win the war with distraction but to create conditions where the war becomes unnecessary.

### Identity Before Behavior
The shift happens at the identity level first. Not "someone who doesn't check their phone" but simply... not being someone who does. The discipline follows from the "who," not the other way around.

Question to sit with: *What does someone who holds work as practice naturally do with their morning? With the gap between tasks?*

### Structure as Container for Surrender
Surrender doesn't mean formlessness. It may require building a structure you can surrender *into*:
- The phone lives somewhere else
- The first two hours have a shape that doesn't include distraction
- The workday has rhythm like a retreat — blocks of focus, transitions marked, periodic landing back in the body

Not discipline imposed, but the natural shape of how someone who holds work as practice moves.

### The Pull as Material
The reach for distraction is itself practice material. What's underneath it? What part is getting something from it? Don't just resist — investigate. But also don't make it a forever war.

### When Difficult Feelings Arise (Tonglen Strategy)
When something lands hard — rejection, tension, the feeling of danger — this is the moment distraction usually wins. Instead:

1. **Notice** the pull toward distraction. Name it.
2. **Turn toward** the sensation. Where is it in the body? Chest? Throat?
3. **Breathe in** — feel it fully. Allow awareness to touch it.
4. **Remember others** — so many people are feeling exactly this right now. You're not alone in it.
5. **Breathe out** — let awareness hold it. Not fixing, just being with.

This transforms the difficult moment from something to escape into material for practice.

### Continuity of Practice
The retreat mind doesn't hold because there's a hard discontinuity between "practice time" and "regular time." The work is closing that gap — not by forcing intensity everywhere, but by bringing awareness to the mundane.
