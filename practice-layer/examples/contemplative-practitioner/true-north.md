# True North

A long-horizon orientation, not a goal with a deadline. The compass for whether a choice moves me toward or away from what I actually care about.

A framing that's stuck with me: **the northstar goes BEHIND me as driving energy — not in front of me as craving for the outcome.** When it's in front, it becomes something to be earned, which turns into conceit and bargaining. When it's behind, it pushes, and I can focus on the next right move without needing the outcome to validate me.

---

## What I'm actually oriented toward

Not "be successful." Not "build a great company." Those are abstractions that reward performance. Underneath them:

**Deep contentment while building.** Becoming the kind of person who is at peace with what is, even while doing hard work in the world. The practice and the work are not separate; one is the ground of the other.

This is the move the old traditions point at — Ajahn Chah: *"Peace is right here, in the heart. It is not found by going somewhere."* The north isn't a destination; it's the orientation that makes any destination survivable.

**Serving people in a way that's reciprocal, not transactional.** Attention to the specific human is worth more than the abstract network. I don't want to become someone whose relationships are downstream of what someone can do for me.

**Mastery with humility.** Working at the edge of what I'm capable of, while knowing the edge is not the self. Being good at the work without the work becoming who I am.

---

## Values across dimensions

Not rules. Orientations. The kind of choice each dimension should lead me toward.

- **Work** — real things shipped for real people, built with care. No vanity metrics. Revenue as a proof of value, not a trophy.
- **Practice** — sitting most mornings, retreats yearly, the work-as-practice frame. If practice is the first thing dropped when work gets hard, the practice wasn't real.
- **Family** — present for my household first. The rest of the world can wait thirty minutes while I'm with the people I share a home with. This is non-negotiable.
- **Body** — strength, sleep, walking. Not peak performance — **floor of reliability** so the mind has something stable to run on.
- **Money** — enough to stop worrying, not so much that managing it becomes its own career. Intentional, not compulsive.
- **Service** — teaching, mentoring, occasional long-form writing. Giving back in proportion to what I've received.

---

## What shows up when I'm drifting

These are the patterns that tell me I've lost the orientation. The model should flag them when it sees them:

- **Busywork as camouflage.** Tier-2 activity that looks productive but avoids the hard exposure move. Refactoring, research, planning, organizing — all real work, all sometimes a hiding place.
- **Outcome-gripping.** Checking for replies, checking for views, checking for reactions. The checking itself is the tell.
- **Relational neglect.** Three weeks without a genuine non-transactional message to anyone outside the immediate household. The LBJ move atrophies fast if I don't use it.
- **Body as an afterthought.** Skipping walks. Eating at the desk. Two nights of short sleep in a row and pretending it's fine.
- **Practice as optional.** Sitting reduced to five minutes so I can "get back to work." The hierarchy inverts; the work is what I do in the strength practice gives me.

---

## How I want the model to hold this

Not as a checklist. As background.

- When I bring a decision, ask whether it moves toward or away from the orientation — quietly, one line, not a lecture.
- When drift patterns show up in recent activity, name the pattern specifically, not generically.
- Never recite this document back to me. It's for you to hold so I don't have to hold it constantly.
- If I'm forcing a move that's clearly out of alignment, name it directly. Compassion without collusion.

Thich Nhat Hanh: *"The present moment is the only moment available to us."* The True North doesn't live in the future. It lives in whether the next small choice reflects it.
