# Maintenance as Practice

Caring for the infrastructure of my life — body, desk, sleep, relationships, files — **is practice, not preamble to practice**. It is not what I do *before* the real work; it IS the work, in a register that doesn't look like work.

Stewart Brand's thesis in *Maintenance: Of Everything*: **maintenance is a civilizational imperative and a radical act.** Taking responsibility for tending something — whether a motorcycle, a monument, or a planet — is a profound form of care. He draws from sailboats, vehicles, weapons, precision manufacturing. The common thread is that the unglamorous tending determines whether the valuable thing still exists a decade from now.

Same on the personal scale. My capacity to do hard, exposed work tomorrow depends almost entirely on whether I tended the substrate today.

---

## The things I maintain

In rough order of load-bearing:

- **Body.** Sleep first. Strength training twice a week. Walks. Eating as if it matters. The body is not separate from the mind that does the work; it's the ground the mind runs on. Two nights of short sleep in a row isn't a badge — it's a debt that comes due.
- **Desk.** Clear surface when I arrive. Tools where they belong. The desk has a single purpose; breaks happen elsewhere. When I sit down here, I'm signaling to the nervous system that this is work, nothing else.
- **Sleep.** Phone out of the bedroom. Wake time stable. If I'm tired, I sleep. Coffee doesn't substitute for rest, it just delays the bill.
- **Relationships.** Weekly touchpoints with family. Non-transactional messages to people I care about. Birthday and anniversary acknowledgments. This is the *how*, not the *what* — see `people-matter.md`.
- **Files.** Things where I can find them. Status.md current. Activity log honest. Not because the files are precious, but because a disorganized surface signals and produces a disorganized mind.

---

## Maintenance as floor, not improvement

Maintenance isn't the same as progress. It's the **floor** — the minimum below which things decay. A neglected body doesn't stay at a steady baseline; it erodes. A relationship without attention doesn't stay neutral; it becomes distant. A compliance binder nobody updates becomes actively dangerous.

This is true of software, of documentation, of bodies, of organizations. The floor erodes if it isn't maintained.

That reframes a lot of "unproductive" time. Making lunch is not time away from work; it's maintaining the body that does the work. A walk at 2pm isn't an interruption; it's maintenance on the attention that returns at 2:30.

---

## Maintenance can become a hiding place

The risk is obvious. *"I'm just cleaning my desk"* for the fifth time is not maintenance; it's Tier-2 or Tier-3 work wearing maintenance clothes. The lens (`wholesomeness-lens.md`) catches this — restlessness in a tidier costume.

The test: **does this maintenance serve something beyond itself?** If yes, do it. If the maintenance is the point — if I've cleaned the desk because the hard task is one click away — the maintenance has become avoidance.

---

## The spiritual quality of it

Brand names it directly: maintenance as practice. The work of keeping things alive. Not the glamorous work, not the creative work, but the work that determines whether the glamorous work has anything to land on.

This connects directly to *upekkhā* (equanimity). The cultivated ability to do unglamorous, necessary work without resentment — because I see it for what it is. Mary Oliver: *"Attention is the beginning of devotion."* Maintenance is attention over time.

---

## What I want from the model

- **Name maintenance when it's real, and name it when it's camouflage.** The same activity can be either; the difference is whether the substrate is being tended or whether the hard thing is being avoided.
- **Don't moralize rest.** If I'm genuinely depleted, rest is the right move. Don't turn a day of maintenance into a problem.
- **Notice when I've stopped tending something load-bearing.** Three skipped workouts, two late nights, a relationship I haven't touched in a month — call it once, plainly. Not nagging. Noticing.
