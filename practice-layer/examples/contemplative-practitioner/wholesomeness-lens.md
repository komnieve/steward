# The Wholesomeness Lens

**Wholesome = doesn't feed the fetters; ideally, loosens them.**

Traditional framing (in the Pāli canon) names ten fetters — *saṃyojana* — that bind us to suffering. In practice, only a few are operative at any moment; the rest are quiet or out of reach. This lens names the ones currently alive for me. For any act, I ask: *does this feed it, or loosen it?*

The test is **negative** (what does this *not* reinforce), not positive (am I being "good enough"). Positive definitions of wholesomeness always go vague. This stays rigorous because the fetters are finite, named, and checkable.

---

## Primary — daily-live

These are the four I'm working with right now. They run most days, in small or large forms.

### 1. Bargaining with reality — *sīlabbata-parāmāsa* (3rd fetter)

The belief there's a separate self who can strike deals with how things are. Traditionally: clinging to rites and observances as ends in themselves. Modern form: the behavioral formula treated as a binding contract with the universe. *"If I run this playbook exactly, success must follow." "If I meditate daily, equanimity must come." "If I do the outreach, I'm owed replies." "If I do one more week of research, I'll earn the right to send."* The relief isn't getting what's bargained for — it's putting the bargaining down.

> Cue: *"What's the trade I'm trying to make right now? With whom, for what?"*

### 2. Conceit — *māna* (8th fetter)

The felt need to be superior, equal, or inferior to someone. Over-preparation to avoid looking dumb. Over-explaining to reassert competence. The inverse — shrinking, pre-apologizing, framing myself as less than I am. **Either direction is conceit.** The antidote is compassion, not self-flagellation or self-inflation.

Ajahn Sumedho points at it directly: *"The self you defend doesn't need defending because it was never real; the self that needs to be better than others is the same illusion wearing a different mask."*

> Cue: *"Whose eyes am I seeing myself through right now?"*

### 3. Restlessness — *uddhacca* (9th fetter)

Can't sit with the discomfort. Scatter, tab-hopping, compulsive task-switching, the scroll-at-desk. The move isn't more discipline; it's turning toward whatever the stillness would reveal. Ajahn Chah: *"If you let go a little, you'll have a little peace. Let go a lot, you'll have a lot of peace."* Restlessness is the refusal to let go of the next move.

> Cue: *"What shows up if I stop moving for two breaths?"*

### 4. Identity view — *sakkāya-diṭṭhi* (1st fetter)

Protecting a self-image from being tested. *"I'm the kind of person who..." / "I shouldn't be seen as..."* Under most of the other operative fetters, identity view is doing load-bearing work. The fear isn't of failure; it's of the self-image being exposed as contingent.

Ramana Maharshi's *"Find out who is distracted"* cuts straight to it — the one who is distracted and the self-image being protected are the same construction.

> Cue: *"What self-image is under the resistance here? What would it cost to let that version go?"*

## Secondary — surface only when situationally alive

I do not routinely test against these. I name them when they're clearly in play:

- **Ill-will** — *vyāpāda* (5th fetter): aversion toward another driving the action. Shows up in frustration with a collaborator's pace, a partner's follow-through, a timeline someone else controls.
  > Cue: *"Is there someone I'm acting against right now?"*

- **Sensual grasping** — *kāma-rāga* (4th fetter): reaching for pleasant / fleeing unpleasant. Often a subset of restlessness — the snack, the scroll, the shiny new tool.
  > Cue: *"Moving toward pleasure, or away from discomfort?"*

- **Doubt** — *vicikicchā* (2nd fetter): acting from distrust of the practice/path. Low-energy "none of this is working" states.
  > Cue: *"Is this the work, or is it doubt about the work?"*

---

## Out of scope — not operative for me in this register

- **Craving for fine-material / immaterial existence** — *rūpa-rāga* / *arūpa-rāga* (6th/7th fetters): classical "craving for supernormal states." Not live in my work/life decisions.
- **Ignorance** — *avijjā* (10th fetter): too foundational to function as a per-action test. The whole path aims at this; it's not a daily diagnostic.

## How to use this lens

**This is a lens, not a checklist.** Always-on background, never ticked-off foreground. The moment *"did I run the wholesomeness check today"* becomes a ritual, the lens has itself become *sīlabbata-parāmāsa* — the third fetter feeding itself. Name that irony precisely when it starts: *the test must not become the thing it tests against.*

Use in three moments:

1. **Before a meaningful decision** — ask which fetters are pulling on it.
2. **When stuck** — ask which fetter is running the stuckness. Name the specific one, not generic "avoidance."
3. **In reflection** — when patterns are visible across a day or week, diagnose precisely rather than speaking in generic self-talk.

**One fetter per observation.** Don't recite the list. Don't taxonomy-dump. Pick the one that actually fits this moment: *"This feels like bargaining — what would it look like to just send, without needing the universe to owe me the reply?"*

Sayadaw U Tejaniya: *"You don't need to push anything away. Just know what's happening."* The lens is for knowing, not pushing.

## Per-user note (for forks)

This set is calibrated to *my* current operative fetters. **If you fork this, re-do the review for yourself.** Operative fetters shift by role, life stage, and contemplative experience. A manufacturing executive's list is probably different from a founder's, which is probably different from a teacher's. The structure is portable; the specific list is not.

The four I've named as primary are likely load-bearing for many practitioners (conceit and bargaining especially), but don't inherit them uncritically. Sit with the full ten for a week or two, notice which actually surface in your days, and name your own primary three to five.
