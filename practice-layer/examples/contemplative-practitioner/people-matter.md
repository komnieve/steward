# People Matter

The relationships I carry are not *context for* the work. They are the work, in a register I want the model to hold explicitly.

**People matter.** Not as nodes in a network, not as pipeline contacts, not as prospects — as *people*. This is a re-valuation, not a technique. I'm building an operating system in which the individual human in front of me is worth attending to, remembering, and showing up for, independent of what they can do for me.

This isn't about becoming a "remembers everyone's name" person as a trick. It's the deeper move underneath: deciding that attention itself is the currency I want to trade in.

---

## Why this matters enough to be its own component

Because the protective part of me — the part that learned early to prefer systems over people — is strong. Specs don't abandon me. Architectures don't leave. But most of the aliveness in my life is in the specific humans I've actually let myself attend to. Extending that is the practice.

And because the relational move is not just ethics — it's also strategy. Robert Caro spent 36 years describing how Lyndon Johnson's political power was *downstream* of the care he took with individual people. The LBJ move is relational sales: remembering the kids' names, sending the *"I was thinking of you"* message three months later, knowing where someone grew up. Most network-building advice has this inverted — it treats people as nodes and then wonders why nothing lands.

---

## The intention field

For each person I want to hold in practice, I name a small intention: **how I want to show up with this person.** Not what I want *from* them. What I want to *be* for them.

Examples from my own list:

- *Someone important to me:* **Be a steady friend. Listen. Don't try to fix.**
- *A collaborator:* **Be a peer, not a manager. Respect the craft. Give clear feedback.**
- *A mentor:* **Show up with concrete progress since last time. Ask real questions, not polite ones.**
- *A family member I sometimes get impatient with:* **Slow down. Match their rhythm. Not mine.**

The intention is not a policy. It's a direction. When I'm drifting out of alignment with someone, it's usually because I forgot the intention I actually hold for them.

---

## The small practice

I keep the discipline light, because heavy structure kills the thing it's meant to support. Monthly, maybe:

- **Read the list.** Notice who I haven't touched in a while.
- **One genuine message.** Not transactional. Not asking for anything. One non-performative note to one person.
- **Update after real conversations.** What did we talk about? What did they say they were worried about? What did I promise to follow up on?
- **Add new people I actually meet and want to keep.** Not everyone. The ones I'd want to know in five years.

This is infrastructure for the valuation, not a substitute for it. If the list ever becomes another place where I track contacts transactionally without actually attending to the humans, I've betrayed the direction and need to stop and reset.

---

## What I want from the model

- **Ask about specific humans by name**, occasionally and warmly. Not as a checklist; as a way to bring them into attention.
- **Flag stale relationships before they become distant.** Three months without contact with someone I named in my list is a signal, not a crisis.
- **Surface the intention when relevant.** If I'm about to write to someone, and my intention for them is "slow down, match their rhythm," a gentle reminder of that before I hit send changes what gets sent.
- **Never treat relationships like pipeline.** The language of CRM does not belong here. If I slip into that frame, call it.

---

## The through-line

This is the same move as `maintenance-as-practice.md` and `work-as-practice.md`: the *how* matters as much as the *what*, and in this case the *how* is **with care, individual by individual, remembering**. That's the whole thing.
