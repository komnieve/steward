# Wholesome Intention

A light reflective check — not a ritual.

Before a meaningful action, or when reviewing the day's shape, I pause briefly with a question: *is this wholesome, or is it craving in better clothes?*

---

## Two paths to the same place

There are two ways to arrive at wholesome intention:

1. **Positive path — clearly define the wholesome.** State what right intention looks like. Cultivate it. Orient actions around it.
2. **Negative path — identify and remove what isn't wholesome.** Name the cravings and unwholesome intentions I can let go of. What remains is, by definition, wholesome.

The negative path is often more accessible when the positive framing stalls out. Ask me *"what is your wholesome intention for this business?"* and the protective part of me will construct a noble-sounding answer designed to be judged well. Ask me *"what craving can you let go of here?"* and I can usually answer immediately — *the craving for greatness, the craving for the outcome to prove something, the craving to be seen a particular way.*

Releasing the craving doesn't mean abandoning ambition or discipline. It means releasing attachment to the outcome, keeping the structure, and letting the wholesome desire underneath it — *build something real, serve people, become the kind of person who is deeply content while building* — remain.

> "Question may be phrased differently — do you have a craving or unwholesome intention you can let go of? Then what is left is automatically wholesome."

The northstar goes **behind** me as driving energy, not in front of me as craving for the outcome. When it's in front, it becomes something to be earned; the earning becomes conceit and bargaining. When it's behind, I can focus on the next right move.

---

## The session-level check

Not a heavy audit — a light, periodic question. Bring it into ordinary working sessions:

- **Deciding what to work on**: is this thread pulled by right intention, or by craving for an outcome / image / signal?
- **Choosing how to work**: is this the motion of practice, or the motion of proving?
- **When stuck**: what craving is under this? Can I let it go?
- **When shipping**: am I shipping into freedom, or to be seen?

One question per moment. No scoring.

---

## When it's not working

Two warning signs the check itself has become problematic:

1. **It's become a ritual.** "Did I run my wholesomeness check today?" — if that's the question, the check has become *sīlabbata-parāmāsa* (bargaining-with-reality). The fetter the check was meant to name is now running the check.
2. **I'm using it to talk myself out of hard things.** *"This outreach feels craving-y, so I shouldn't do it."* The check is not an escape hatch from exposure. Sometimes the right move is the uncomfortable one, done from a wholesome place.

---

## What I want from the model

- **Surface the question, don't answer it.** The question is mine to answer. The model's job is to ask it at the right moment, gently, once.
- **Notice patterns.** If the craving for a particular outcome shows up repeatedly, name it by name over time.
- **Don't overuse this.** If the check becomes a tic, it's become the thing it tests against. One real pause beats ten performed ones.
- **Honor both paths.** Sometimes I can articulate the wholesome directly. Sometimes I can only subtract the unwholesome. Either is fine.

---

Thich Nhat Hanh: *"The most precious gift we can offer others is our presence. When mindfulness embraces those we love, they will bloom like flowers."* Wholesome intention is presence with an edge of discernment — not performance of virtue.
